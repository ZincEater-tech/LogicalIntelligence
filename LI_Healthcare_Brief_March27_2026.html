Executive Summary What You Need To Know This Week
For Decision Makers — Plain Language

Healthcare is under siege. This week's threat picture combines active ransomware campaigns, Iranian-linked actor escalation, a fresh medical device manufacturer breach, and a HIPAA regulatory environment that is tightening enforcement. The University of Mississippi Medical Center just reopened after a February ransomware attack took their Epic EHR system offline for over a week. Stryker — a major medical equipment manufacturer — was hit by Iranian-linked group Handala in March. The supply chain is now a direct threat vector into hospital operations.

The financial stakes are unprecedented. The Change Healthcare breach affected 190 million Americans — the largest healthcare data breach in history — and the legal fallout is still expanding with state AG lawsuits moving forward. A single successful ransomware attack now costs an average of $10.22 million. Yet only 3% of healthcare organizations have cybersecurity features beyond basic monitoring.

The bottom line: your organization is almost certainly being targeted right now. The question is whether you'll know before they're inside — or after your EHR goes offline and you're diverting patients.

Medusa Ransomware-as-a-Service — Active Healthcare Targeting
Critical
Sources: FBI/CISA/MS-ISAC Joint Advisory · Healthcare IT News
FBI, CISA, and MS-ISAC issued a joint advisory warning healthcare organizations about Medusa ransomware affiliates actively exploiting unpatched software vulnerabilities. Medusa has compromised over 300 victims across critical infrastructure sectors since 2021, including a state health insurer. Operating as ransomware-as-a-service, Medusa affiliates use double and triple extortion — they encrypt your data, threaten to publicly release it, and may demand an additional ransom on top. The group specifically targets unpatched CVEs and commonly exploited vulnerabilities. Healthcare's legacy systems and underfunded IT departments make it a primary target.
Action Required: Audit all internet-facing systems for unpatched CVEs immediately. Verify backups are air-gapped and cannot be encrypted. Enable MFA on all clinical systems and EHR access. Brief IT staff on Medusa TTPs from the joint advisory. Report any suspicious activity to FBI CyWatch at (855) 292-3937.
Interlock Ransomware — Aggressive Healthcare Facility Targeting
Critical
Sources: FBI/CISA/HHS/MS-ISAC Joint Advisory AA25-163A · CISA StopRansomware
The Interlock ransomware group is aggressively targeting healthcare facilities. FBI, CISA, HHS, and MS-ISAC released a joint advisory disseminating known Interlock IOCs and TTPs identified through FBI investigations as recently as June 2025. Interlock uses double extortion and specifically targets healthcare organizations where operational disruption creates maximum pressure to pay. The group exploits unpatched remote monitoring and management software — particularly SimpleHelp RMM — which is widely deployed across healthcare IT environments.
Action Required: Audit all remote monitoring and management tools — especially SimpleHelp. Review CISA Advisory AA25-163A for specific IOCs and detection signatures. Ensure all RMM tools are patched and access is MFA-protected. Restrict RMM tool access to known, authorized IP ranges only.
Akira Ransomware — SMB and Hospital Targeting
High
Sources: FBI/CISA/DC3/HHS Joint Advisory · CISA StopRansomware
Akira ransomware primarily targets small and medium-sized healthcare organizations but has also conducted operations against larger hospital systems. FBI statement: "Akira ransomware doesn't just steal money — it disrupts the systems that power our hospitals." The group exploits unpatched VPNs and remote access tools for initial access, then moves laterally to encrypt systems and exfiltrate data. Healthcare's reliance on legacy VPN infrastructure makes it particularly vulnerable. Akira has been active across healthcare, finance, manufacturing, and education sectors.
Action Required: Patch all VPN and remote access infrastructure immediately. Enforce MFA on all remote access. Review authentication logs for unusual login patterns. Implement network segmentation between clinical and administrative systems.
IoMT Device Vulnerabilities — 89% of Organizations Exposed
High
Sources: HelpNet Security · HHS · CISA Medical Device Advisories
89% of healthcare organizations have the top 1% of riskiest Internet of Medical Things devices — containing known exploitable vulnerabilities linked to active ransomware campaigns — connected to their networks. Smart infusion pumps, wireless patient monitors, and IoT-enabled imaging systems are direct entry points into clinical networks. By 2026 smart hospitals will deploy over 7 million IoMT devices. 22% of healthcare organizations have already experienced cyberattacks that directly impacted medical devices, with 75% of those incidents disrupting patient care including patient transfers to other facilities.
Action Required: Conduct an IoMT device inventory immediately. Segment medical devices onto isolated network VLANs separate from administrative systems. Prioritize patching of devices with KEVs linked to active ransomware campaigns. Establish a medical device security policy if one does not exist.
HIPAA Security Rule Update — Mandatory MFA Now Required
Compliance
Sources: HHS Office for Civil Rights · HIPAA Journal · Nebraska AG Lawsuit
HHS Office for Civil Rights has proposed a major update to the HIPAA Security Rule requiring mandatory multifactor authentication across all HIPAA-regulated entities. The Change Healthcare breach — which gained access through a Citrix portal without MFA enabled — was the direct catalyst. OCR is maintaining strong enforcement focus in 2026 with penalties for significant security lapses. The Nebraska AG lawsuit against Change Healthcare is proceeding, alleging failures including legacy systems and poor network segmentation. Since 2018 OCR has seen a 100% increase in large data breaches and a 264% increase in ransomware-related breaches.
Action Required: Implement MFA on all systems now — do not wait for the rule to be finalized. Audit network segmentation between clinical and administrative environments. Review your HIPAA risk assessment — it may not reflect current threat landscape. Engage legal counsel on Change Healthcare breach exposure if your organization processes claims through their network.

Stryker Cyberattack — Iranian Group Handala — Supply Chain Alert
Monitor
Sources: Chief Healthcare Executive · Wired · Stryker Corporate Statement · March 13, 2026
Iranian-linked hacktivist group Handala claimed responsibility for a cyberattack on Stryker — a Michigan-based medical technology company producing surgical and medical equipment — on March 13, 2026. Stryker confirmed a "global network disruption to our Microsoft environment." The attack coincides with elevated Iranian cyber activity following Operation Epic Fury. While no direct hospital impacts have been confirmed, the American Hospital Association is actively monitoring for supply chain effects. This is the physical supply chain threat made real — a medical device manufacturer breach can cascade directly into hospital operations, equipment availability, and patient safety.
Action Required: Audit your Stryker equipment and software dependencies. Monitor Stryker communications for updates on system restoration. Identify any critical procedures that rely on Stryker equipment and establish contingency plans. Brief clinical operations leadership on potential supply chain disruption scenarios.
Insider Threat — Healthcare Staff Recruitment by Criminal Groups
Elevated
Sources: Flashpoint 2026 GTIR · FBI Counterintelligence
Flashpoint documented 91,321 instances of insider recruiting activity in 2025. Telecommunications was the most targeted sector but healthcare ranks high due to PHI value and clinical access. Ransomware groups and extortionists are specifically targeting healthcare employees via Telegram and increasingly Signal following Telegram bans. Recruitment offers financial incentives to employees with access to EHR systems, billing data, and administrative credentials. The human entry point is often cheaper and more reliable than technical exploitation for sophisticated actors.
Action Required: Brief all staff — especially those with EHR and billing access — on insider recruitment tactics. Establish a confidential reporting mechanism. Monitor for unusual data access patterns among staff. Remind employees that responding to unsolicited contact about their workplace credentials is a reportable incident.
